The Bharatiya Sakshya (Second) Bill, 2023: Recasting India’s Law of Evidence

The Bharatiya Sakshya (Second) Bill, 2023 forms part of the Government of India’s ambitious effort to comprehensively modernise the country’s criminal justice framework. Alongside the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, 2023 (replacing the Indian Penal Code) and the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita, 2023 (replacing the Code of Criminal Procedure), this Bill seeks to overhaul the Indian Evidence Act, 1872—a colonial-era statute that has governed evidentiary rules for over 150 years.

For businesses, institutions, and individuals operating in India, this reform has far-reaching implications for litigation strategy, compliance, and the adjudication of disputes.

Key Features of the Bill

1. Digital Evidence at the Core

  • The Bill explicitly recognises electronic and digital records—including emails, servers, cloud storage, SMS, instant messaging, and digital photographs—as primary evidence, giving them parity with traditional documentary evidence.
  • This shift is critical in an era of technology-driven commerce and governance.

2. Streamlined Provisions

  • The Bill reduces the number of sections from 167 in the old Act to 170, consolidating and modernising archaic language.
  • Redundant provisions have been removed to align the law with contemporary practice.

3. Admissibility of Electronic Records

  • Provisions relating to admissibility of digital evidence, earlier governed under Section 65B of the Evidence Act, are retained with greater clarity.
  • Certificates for electronic evidence are mandatory to ensure authenticity, but the Bill aims to remove procedural uncertainty that has previously led to inconsistent judicial interpretation.

4. Presumptions in Law

  • New presumptions have been introduced for certain categories of electronic evidence (e.g., secure electronic records and digital signatures).
  • This is expected to reduce evidentiary disputes in both criminal and commercial proceedings.

5. Witness Testimony & Expert Evidence

  • Broader scope for expert evidence, especially in forensic science and digital forensics.
  • Virtual and video-linked testimony is recognised, facilitating efficiency in judicial proceedings.

Implications for Businesses and Institutions

  • Compliance and Governance: Companies must strengthen digital recordkeeping, ensuring that electronic communications and documentation are systematically preserved for potential use in litigation or regulatory investigations.
  • Litigation Strategy: Legal teams will need to revisit their evidentiary practices, especially in commercial disputes, white-collar crime investigations, and data-related litigatio
  • Technology Adoption: The Bill encourages greater reliance on digital tools in dispute resolution, opening opportunities for tech-enabled compliance and e-discovery solutions.
  • Cross-Border Relevance: For international corporations, the recognition of digital records provides greater certainty when dealing with evidence generated and stored outside India but relied upon in Indian proceedings.

By recognising the centrality of digital evidence and introducing clearer frameworks for admissibility, the Bill strengthens both criminal justice delivery and commercial dispute resolution.

Conclusion

The Bharatiya Sakshya (Second) Bill, 2023, represents a decisive shift toward a modern, technology-oriented evidentiary regime. For businesses and institutions, it underscores the growing importance of data integrity, forensic preparedness, and legal compliance in the digital age.

As India undertakes this historic reform, legal strategies must evolve in tandem—ensuring that stakeholders remain both compliant and litigation-ready in a rapidly changing legal environment.
